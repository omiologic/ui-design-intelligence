# The 10 Laws of Copywriting

These laws are useful as a practical checklist for writing website copy, landing pages, product pages, ads, emails, and prototype content.
They are especially helpful when copy needs to support a user journey, explain value clearly, and move the reader toward action.

---

## Law #1: You must learn how people make decisions.

Copywriting is not only about writing beautiful sentences.
It is about understanding how people decide what to believe, what to trust, and what to do next.

Most people do not make decisions only through logic.
They make decisions through a mix of emotion, instinct, habit, fear, desire, urgency, memory, and social influence.

Good copy understands this.

A customer usually asks silent questions while reading:

- Is this for me?
- Do I trust this?
- Does this solve my problem?
- Is this worth the cost?
- What happens if I do nothing?
- What happens if I choose the wrong option?
- What should I do next?

Your copy should guide the reader through those questions.

People often decide emotionally first, then justify logically afterward.
That does not mean your copy should manipulate them.
It means your copy should respect how real decision-making works.

A strong copywriter understands:

- the user’s current pain
- the user’s desired outcome
- the risk they feel
- the objections they carry
- the proof they need
- the next step they are willing to take

For website and prototype design, this law affects section order.

A page should not randomly present information.
It should match how the user makes a decision.

For example, a medical product page may need this sequence:

1. Identify the user’s problem.
2. Show that the product understands the problem.
3. Explain the solution.
4. Compare options.
5. Provide proof.
6. Address concerns.
7. Offer a clear next step.

Bad copy assumes the user is ready to buy immediately.
Good copy understands the user may need confidence before action.

Use this law when planning:

- hero sections
- product pages
- service pages
- pricing sections
- comparison tables
- FAQ sections
- inquiry dialogs
- onboarding flows

The main question is:

> What does the user need to believe before they are ready to act?

If the copy answers that question, it becomes much stronger.

## Law #2: You must uncover the selfish benefit.

People care about features, but they act on benefits.
A feature explains what something is.
A benefit explains why it matters to the user.

The “selfish benefit” does not mean the customer is bad or shallow.
It means every user reads copy through the lens of personal relevance.

They are asking:

- What does this do for me?
- How does this save my time?
- How does this reduce my stress?
- How does this make me look better?
- How does this help me avoid a mistake?
- How does this help me make money?
- How does this make my life easier?
- How does this protect me?

A common copywriting mistake is stopping at the feature.

Example:

> This headlamp has an external battery pack.

That is a feature.

A stronger benefit:

> The external battery pack reduces weight on your head and neck during long procedures.

That explains why the feature matters.

Even stronger:

> Stay focused through long procedures with less neck strain from a lighter head-mounted frame.

Now the copy connects the product to the user’s daily experience.

A useful formula:

```txt
Feature → Practical benefit → Emotional benefit
```

Example:

```txt
Feature:
Adjustable spot lighting

Practical benefit:
Focus light exactly where you need it

Emotional benefit:
Work with more control and less uncertainty
```

The selfish benefit is often not the product itself.
It is what the product helps the user become, avoid, or achieve.

For B2B copy, selfish benefits may include:

- saving time
- reducing training
- avoiding costly mistakes
- looking professional
- making better decisions
- increasing revenue
- reducing support requests
- improving client trust

For healthcare or dental copy, selfish benefits may include:

- feeling safe
- reducing anxiety
- understanding options
- trusting the provider
- avoiding pain
- getting clear next steps

For UI and prototype copy, every section should have a benefit role.

A feature grid should not only list features.
Each card should answer:

> Why does this matter to the user?

Weak:

> Dual light modes

Better:

> Choose broad diffused light or focused spot light depending on your procedure.

Strong:

> Use the right lighting style for the way you actually work.

The law is simple:

> Do not make the user translate features into value. Do that work for them.

## Law #3: You must learn how to tell a great story.

A story gives copy a sense of movement.
It helps the reader understand where they are, what problem exists, why it matters, and what solution can change the situation.

Good website copy often follows a simple narrative:

1. Here is the problem.
2. Here is why it matters.
3. Here is what usually makes it difficult.
4. Here is a better way.
5. Here is why this solution works.
6. Here is what to do next.

Storytelling does not always mean writing a long dramatic story.
It can be a simple structure that creates emotional and logical flow.

A page without story feels like a collection of disconnected blocks.
A page with story feels intentional.

For example, a product landing page might tell this story:

```txt
Choosing the right medical light is difficult.
Different procedures require different lighting.
Buying multiple lights is expensive and confusing.
This product simplifies the decision.
Here are the options.
Here is how to choose.
Here is proof.
Here is how to inquire.
```

That is a story.

The reader moves from confusion to clarity.

A strong story usually includes:

- a clear audience
- a recognizable problem
- a meaningful consequence
- a better solution
- proof that the solution works
- a clear next action

For prototype design, storytelling helps decide the order of sections.

A basic storytelling page structure:

```txt
Hero
Problem
Solution
Benefits
How it works
Proof
FAQ
CTA
```

For a service business:

```txt
Hero
Pain point
Service overview
Why choose us
Process
Testimonials
FAQ
Booking CTA
```

For a product page:

```txt
Hero
Product value
Feature explanation
Use cases
Comparison
Specifications
Reviews
FAQ
Purchase or inquiry CTA
```

Storytelling also improves section copy.

Weak heading:

> Our Features

Stronger heading:

> Everything you need to choose with confidence

Weak CTA:

> Submit

Stronger CTA:

> Get Product Guidance

A story makes the user feel guided, not sold to.

The key question is:

> What journey should the reader experience from the first headline to the final CTA?

If each section moves the reader forward, the copy becomes more persuasive.

## Law #4: You must write like you talk.

Good copy sounds human.
It should feel like a clear conversation, not a corporate announcement.

Many websites sound unnatural because they use language people would never say out loud.

Weak:

> We provide innovative solutions designed to optimize your operational workflow.

Better:

> We help your team work faster with tools that are easier to manage.

Writing like you talk does not mean being sloppy.
It means being clear, natural, and easy to understand.

A good test:

> Would I say this sentence to a real customer?

If not, rewrite it.

Conversational copy often uses:

- shorter sentences
- familiar words
- direct explanations
- active voice
- simple transitions
- natural rhythm

It avoids:

- corporate filler
- vague claims
- unnecessary jargon
- overcomplicated sentence structure
- empty buzzwords

Examples of phrases to question:

```txt
innovative solutions
cutting-edge technology
seamless experience
robust platform
next-generation system
best-in-class
revolutionary
```

These phrases are not always wrong, but they are often lazy.
They sound impressive without explaining anything.

Better copy is usually more specific.

Instead of:

> Seamless product experience

Try:

> Compare models, choose accessories, and request support from one place.

Instead of:

> Innovative healthcare solution

Try:

> A lighter headlamp setup designed to reduce neck strain during long procedures.

Writing like you talk also helps with interface copy.

Weak:

> Invalid input detected.

Better:

> Please enter a valid email address.

Weak:

> Authentication failed.

Better:

> We could not sign you in. Check your email and password.

For brand voice, “write like you talk” does not mean every brand should sound casual.
A professional brand can still sound human.

Professional and human:

> Choose the model that fits your procedure, comfort needs, and preferred lighting style.

Too stiff:

> Select an applicable model according to procedural requirements and illumination preference.

The goal is clarity.

The reader should never have to decode your copy.

## Law #5: You must use your customer's words, not yours.

Customers have their own language for describing their problems.
Good copy uses that language.

Businesses often describe products from the inside:

```txt
advanced optical system
optimized configuration
integrated support framework
proprietary workflow engine
```

Customers usually describe problems from the outside:

```txt
I need better light.
My neck gets tired.
I do not know which model to choose.
I want something reliable.
I do not want to waste money on the wrong option.
```

The customer’s words are usually clearer and more persuasive.

This law is especially important for:

- headlines
- product descriptions
- FAQ questions
- comparison sections
- form labels
- support pages
- onboarding copy

A good copywriter listens before writing.

Useful sources of customer language:

- reviews
- support emails
- sales calls
- survey responses
- search queries
- Reddit/forum discussions
- client conversations
- product questions
- competitor reviews
- FAQ submissions

For example, a business might say:

> Ergonomic illumination platform

But the customer may say:

> I need a headlamp that does not hurt my neck after long procedures.

The second version is more useful.

Customer language often reveals:

- real pain points
- objections
- desired outcomes
- buying triggers
- emotional concerns
- comparison criteria
- words users search for

For UI design, this affects navigation and section labels.

A company might want:

```txt
Solutions
Capabilities
Ecosystem
```

But users may understand:

```txt
Products
Services
Pricing
Support
```

The best label is not always the most creative one.
It is the one users recognize fastest.

This law also prevents copy from becoming too self-centered.

Company-centered:

> We are proud to offer premium dental technology.

Customer-centered:

> Find the tools that help your clinic work with more comfort and control.

The question is:

> Would the customer use these words to describe what they want?

If not, the copy may need translation.

## Law #6: You must write short, choppy copy.

Short copy is easier to scan.
Most users do not read websites like books.
They skim, pause, compare, and decide where to click.

Short, choppy copy creates rhythm.
It helps important ideas stand out.

This does not mean every sentence must be tiny.
It means the copy should avoid long, heavy blocks that slow users down.

Weak:

> Our company provides a comprehensive range of lighting products designed to accommodate the various needs of dental and medical professionals who require reliable visibility during different kinds of procedures.

Better:

> Different procedures need different lighting.
> Our headlamps help you choose between broad diffused light and focused spot light.

The second version is easier to read.

Short copy works well for:

- hero sections
- feature cards
- product benefits
- CTA sections
- email copy
- ad copy
- mobile screens
- onboarding flows
- dialogs and drawers

A useful rhythm:

```txt
Short sentence.
Short sentence.
Slightly longer sentence with more context.
Short CTA.
```

Example:

```txt
Choose the right light faster.
Compare diffused and adjustable spot options.
Find the setup that fits your work style, comfort needs, and procedure type.
Get Product Guidance
```

Short copy does not mean shallow copy.
It means dense copy is broken into readable pieces.

For technical products, you may need detail.
But detail should be structured.

Use:

- bullets
- tables
- short paragraphs
- labels
- comparison rows
- FAQ items
- progressive disclosure

Avoid:

- long paragraphs
- stacked claims
- multiple ideas in one sentence
- vague intros before the main point

Bad:

> In order to provide users with the ability to better understand the differences between our available products, we have created a comparison guide that includes information about features, specifications, and possible use cases.

Better:

> Compare each model by light type, battery setup, comfort, and use case.

The law is about respect.

Short copy respects the reader’s time.

## Law #7: You must be specific.

Specific copy is more believable than vague copy.

Weak copy says:

```txt
high quality
easy to use
great results
better experience
advanced technology
trusted solution
```

Specific copy explains what that means.

Better:

```txt
123 g head-mounted frame
two light types: diffused and adjustable spot
external belt battery pack
compatible with Gen 2 and Gen 2S only
charges in about 1 hour 40 minutes
```

Specificity builds trust.

It helps the user understand the real value.
It also reduces uncertainty.

For product copy, specificity can include:

- measurements
- compatibility
- use cases
- materials
- timing
- process steps
- included items
- limitations
- comparison criteria
- warranty or support details

For service copy, specificity can include:

- who the service is for
- what is included
- how the process works
- how long it takes
- what happens next
- what the client needs to provide
- what problem is solved

Specific copy also improves CTAs.

Weak:

> Learn More

Better:

> Compare Headlamp Models

Weak:

> Get Started

Better:

> Request a Product Recommendation

Weak:

> Contact Us

Better:

> Ask About Compatibility

Specific copy is not always longer.
Often it is shorter because it removes vague filler.

Weak:

> We offer a wide range of innovative solutions for modern dental professionals.

Better:

> Choose headlamps, loupes, batteries, and replacement parts for clinical use.

Specificity is especially important in regulated or professional industries.
If you cannot support a claim, do not make it.

Instead of:

> The best lighting system for dentists

Try:

> A headlamp system designed for broad illumination, focused lighting, and long-procedure comfort.

The question is:

> Can the reader picture exactly what this means?

If not, make it more specific.

## Law #8: You must nail the headline.

The headline is the entrance to the page, section, email, or ad.
If the headline is weak, many users will not continue.

A good headline quickly answers:

- What is this?
- Who is it for?
- Why should I care?
- What problem does it solve?
- What outcome does it promise?

A headline does not need to explain everything.
But it must create enough clarity or curiosity to keep the reader moving.

Weak headline:

> Welcome to Our Website

Better:

> Professional dental lighting designed for clearer procedures

Weak headline:

> Our Products

Better:

> Choose the headlamp setup that fits your procedure

Weak headline:

> Better Care Starts Here

Better:

> Gentle dental care for patients who want clear answers

Different headline types:

### Benefit headline

```txt
Work with clearer visibility and less neck strain.
```

### Problem headline

```txt
Choosing the right clinical light should not be this complicated.
```

### Comparison headline

```txt
Diffused or focused light? Compare before you choose.
```

### Audience headline

```txt
Lighting systems built for dental professionals.
```

### Outcome headline

```txt
Find the right setup before your next procedure.
```

For prototypes, headlines are critical because they make the mockup feel real.

A wireframe can say:

```txt
Hero headline goes here
```

But a prototype needs:

```txt
Choose medical lighting with more confidence
```

A strong headline should be:

- clear
- specific
- relevant
- easy to scan
- connected to the user’s goal

Avoid headlines that are too clever before they are clear.

Clever:

> Light Years Ahead

Clearer:

> Compare professional headlamps by light type, comfort, and battery setup

Sometimes you can combine both:

> Light Years Ahead in Clinical Comfort

But clarity should come first.

The headline sets the direction for everything below it.

If the headline is strong, the rest of the copy has a better chance to work.

## Law #9: You must back everything up with social proof.

People trust claims more when they are supported by evidence.

Social proof shows that others have used, trusted, bought, reviewed, recommended, or benefited from the product or service.

Common types of social proof:

- testimonials
- reviews
- case studies
- ratings
- customer logos
- before/after examples
- usage numbers
- media mentions
- certifications
- expert endorsements
- professional affiliations
- client results
- user-generated content

A claim alone can feel like marketing.

Claim:

> Our product is trusted by professionals.

Supported claim:

> Used by dental professionals across private clinics, surgical offices, and training environments.

Even stronger if true:

> Trusted by over 500 clinics across the U.S.

Only use proof you can support.

Social proof is especially important when:

- the product is expensive
- the service is personal
- the industry is trust-sensitive
- the user feels risk
- the buyer needs confidence
- competitors look similar
- claims are hard to evaluate alone

For healthcare and professional services, social proof should be careful and credible.

Useful proof sections:

```txt
Testimonials
Clinic logos
Professional use cases
Case studies
Before/after gallery
Review snippets
Certification badges
Warranty/support notes
```

Social proof also belongs near CTAs.

If a user is about to act, proof can reduce hesitation.

Example:

```txt
CTA Section:
Still choosing the right model?
Talk with our team before you order.

Proof nearby:
Used by dental professionals who need reliable visibility through long procedures.
```

For prototypes, social proof makes pages feel much more realistic.

Instead of placeholder testimonial:

> “Great product.” — Customer

Use realistic structure:

> “The lighter frame made long appointments more comfortable for our team.”
> — Clinical user, private dental practice

The law is not only “add testimonials.”
The deeper law is:

> Do not ask the user to believe unsupported claims.

Back up your message.

## Law #10: You must address objections upfront.

Every buyer has objections.

They may not say them out loud, but they are thinking them.

Common objections:

- Is this too expensive?
- Will this work for me?
- Is it compatible with what I already own?
- Is this difficult to use?
- What if I choose the wrong option?
- Can I return it?
- How long does it take?
- Is support available?
- Is this trustworthy?
- Do I need this right now?

Bad copy ignores objections.
Good copy answers them before they block action.

This is especially important for product pages, pricing pages, service pages, and inquiry flows.

Objections can be handled through:

- FAQ sections
- comparison tables
- support notes
- guarantee copy
- compatibility notes
- shipping/return details
- process explanations
- “who this is for” sections
- “not sure?” guidance
- product recommendation dialogs

Example objection:

> I do not know which headlamp model to choose.

Copy response:

> Not sure which model fits your work? Compare diffused and adjustable spot lighting, or contact us for guidance before ordering.

Example objection:

> Is this compatible with my current product?

Copy response:

> This battery is compatible with Gen 2 and Gen 2S models only. It is not compatible with Gen 3 headlamps.

Example objection:

> Will this be comfortable for long use?

Copy response:

> The external belt battery setup reduces weight on the head-mounted frame for long procedures.

Addressing objections does not make the product look weaker.
It makes the brand look honest.

A good FAQ section is not filler.
It is a conversion tool.

Strong FAQ questions sound like real customer questions:

```txt
Which model is right for me?
Is this compatible with my current battery?
How long does the battery last?
Can I order replacement parts?
What should I do if my light needs repair?
```

Weak FAQ questions sound like internal marketing:

```txt
Why are we the best?
What makes our company innovative?
How do we provide excellence?
```

For prototypes, objection handling helps create realistic flows.

A product guide dialog can ask:

```txt
What type of procedures do you perform most often?
Do you prefer broad light or focused control?
Are you looking for a lighter head-mounted setup?
```

That is objection handling through interaction.

The main question is:

> What might stop the user from taking action?

Answer that before the final CTA.
