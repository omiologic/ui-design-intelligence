# Hierarchy, Density, And Register

Structural hierarchy decides what the user should understand first. Density
decides how much the user can responsibly act on at once. Register describes the
page's structural mode, not its visual style.

## Registers

### Marketing

Primary job: establish promise, credibility, and action.

Structural bias:

- Strong first-viewport orientation.
- Early proof.
- Few competing primary actions.
- Sections ordered by persuasion and objection handling.

Why: marketing pages lose users when the promise and next action are ambiguous.

### Product Or Utility

Primary job: help the user complete a task or inspect state.

Structural bias:

- Dense but grouped controls.
- Current state before explanation.
- Clear empty, loading, error, and success states.
- Persistent context for repeated work.

Why: utility pages fail when they behave like brochures or hide operational
controls behind narrative structure.

### Editorial

Primary job: make an argument, teach, or support comprehension.

Structural bias:

- Scannable headings.
- Linear or nested progression.
- Evidence and examples near claims.
- Related next steps after comprehension.

Why: editorial pages fail when they bury the argument or flatten the hierarchy.

## Density Rules

- Increase density when the user compares, monitors, filters, or repeats work.
- Decrease density when the user is deciding, learning, or committing.
- Group dense controls by job, not by implementation source.
- Do not use low density to hide missing content purpose.

## Hierarchy Rules

- One primary action per decision moment.
- Headings should reveal the section job.
- Proof should sit close to the claim it supports.
- Secondary actions should not visually or structurally compete with the primary
  path in the blueprint.
