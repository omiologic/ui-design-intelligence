# Page Section Ordering

A page sequence is a decision path, not a list of available components. Order
sections by the user's next required belief or action. If a section does not
change what the user understands, trusts, compares, or can do, it does not earn
a place in the blueprint.

## Decision Flow

1. Name the primary user decision.
2. Put orientation before persuasion: what is this, who is it for, and what can
   the user do next?
3. Put the first proof before the first complex commitment. Users need evidence
   before forms, pricing decisions, demos, or account creation.
4. Place comparison material only after the offer or task is understood.
5. Place objections near the point where they naturally block progress.
6. End with a clear action or continuation path, not a decorative closing block.

## Common Page Orders

### Marketing Or Conversion Page

1. Hero with clear offer, audience, and primary action.
2. Proof or trust signal that makes the promise credible.
3. Feature or benefit sections ordered by buyer priority.
4. Comparison, process, pricing, or eligibility details.
5. Objection handling such as FAQ, reviews, guarantees, or support.
6. Final CTA tied to the same primary action.

Why: conversion pages fail when they ask for commitment before the user
understands the promise, believes it, and sees a next step.

### Utility Or Product Workflow Page

1. Current state, task title, or operational context.
2. Primary controls or data needed to act.
3. Secondary details, filters, or supporting context.
4. Empty, loading, error, and success states.
5. Help, audit trail, or escalation path.

Why: utility pages fail when explanation displaces the task. The page should
reduce time-to-action and preserve recoverability.

### Editorial Or Content Page

1. Topic, promise, and reader orientation.
2. Scannable structure or table of contents when length demands it.
3. Content sections ordered by comprehension, not internal priority.
4. Supporting evidence, examples, or citations.
5. Related next step.

Why: editorial pages fail when hierarchy hides the argument or forces readers to
read linearly when scanning is the likely behavior.

## Anti-Patterns

- Equal-weight sections from top to bottom: no decision path is being modeled.
- CTA before comprehension: the page asks for action before establishing why.
- Proof buried after details: the page makes claims without early credibility.
- FAQ as a dump: objections should be near the decision they unblock.
